Responsibilities: I was part of a team of 4 student researchers, one lab coordinator, and one full time ux designer. All student researchers participated throughout the research process from May to July 2019. Some of my responsibilities included conducting usability studies, affinity diagram analysis, presenting findings, and report writing.



Background

UBC UX Lab is a hands-on program under UBC Student Communication Services where students learn about user experience and conduct experiments to improve the services that UBC provides its students.

I was part of the summer 2019 cohort and I worked on two projects during my time as a UX student researcher, including conducting usability studies on the Transfer credit search tool and the “What can I do with my Science Degree” webpage.

I will be focusing on the Transfer Credit Search tool usability study below. You can read more about the "What can I do with my Science Degree” project here.

The Transfer Credit Search Tool was launched on June 2, 2019 by UBC Student Communication Services to assist advisors and students. Prior to the launch, three phases of usability testing were already performed on the tool by UBC staff. Our goal was to understand how stakeholders are navigating the tool and to find if there are pain points.





Research Question

How are students and stakeholders navigating the newly launched UBC Transfer Credit Search Tool?






Research Methodology

To evaluate the tool, we ran remote moderated scenario tests on different stakeholders that consisted of a pre-screen questionnaire, a scenario task, and follow up questions.

Usability study flow:

1. Pre-screen Questionnaire: this allowed us to better understand the background of each user, such as what school they were transferring from, and what they were studying.

2. Scenario Task: stakeholders were asked to look up 2 courses in the tool and explain what the credits transfer to at UBC using the talk aloud method. This allowed us to find the pain points in the user flow and take notice of certain functions that were not as intuitive.

3. Follow-up Questions: this allowed us to understand why the user may have experienced certain issues and investigate the root cause of the pain points that arose during the scenario task.



Data Analysis

As multiple pre-launch testings had already been conducted, we tested a smaller sample of 7 participants (4 advisors, 3 students). To analyze the data, we conducted an affinity diagram analysis to categorize similar patterns and trends.



Key Findings

1. Error message confusing: Users were unsure whether the error ‘No Results Found’ meant the course would not transfer at all or was still awaiting evaluation. Users were confused by the vagueness of the error message and felt further clarification was needed.

2. Course code inconsistencies in UBC database: The tool showed the course would not transfer when the user indicated it was supposed to, or the course code was not the same between UBC and other school databases. This finding allowed us to conclude that there were certain inaccuracies within the UBC database, creating mistrust in the users.

3. 'Change Search' button: This button was meant to be an easy way for users to return to the search page without having to re-input general information such as institution name - users found the button difficult to find or expected it to be somewhere else.

For a more detailed look at our results, please take a look at our presentation.



Reflection

What I learned

1. Importantce of Piloting
As this was the first usability study I have ever conducted, I really felt the importance of piloting after planning out the test flow. The test flow sounded perfectly fine in my head, but in reality there were missing pieces such as testing equipment and unclear description in the scenario task that needed clarification. Having one or two people pilot the test flow really helped us flesh out all the nitty gritty details which made the actual testing a lot smoother.

2. Usability Study Protocol
There were many things I found particularly interesting about usability studies, such as having to tell the participant that they should not be afraid to critique the design or tool, and the rigorous consent and privacy regulations regarding user data. These were things that I had never thought about but made a lot of sense and helped me be more aware in my own daily life about saving data on the internet.

Limitations

Some limitations we have in this study was the lack of participants. Although the tool had already undergone three phases of pre-launch usability testing, we would have benefited from a larger sample size. Of the student participants that were tested, all three of them were transferring into the Faculty of Science, we may have gained more representative data and insight and if we ran tests on students transferring into other faculties.

Future Direction

Some improvements that can be made for a better user experience would be to make the ‘Change Search’ button more obvious or put it in a more identifiable location, specifying error messages, improving data reliability, and increasing exposure by improving the SEO of this tool.
