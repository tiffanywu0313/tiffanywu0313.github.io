Optimizing information architecture for tax exemption software (CertManager)

Team: Senior UX Designer (Supervisor), Me (UX Intern), Engineering Manager, Dev(s)

Tools: Figma, Figjam, Dovetail, SurveyMonkey, Pendo, Teams, Competitive Analysis, User Interviews, Qualitative Analysis, Thematic Coding

Duration: 4 weeks

Responsibilities: In this project, I conducted competitive analysis, surveys, and interviews with product stakeholders/users to understand how customers are using CertManager, their most common tasks, and pain points when using the software. Then I analyzed the data to propose design solutions to the team.

Background

CertManager is a centralized electronic repository for sale tax exemptions. Customers can be tax exempt if they are buying supplies to resell, if they are non-profit or other criteria. CertManager helps businesses manage their customer’s exemptions by collecting, reporting, and producing customer exemption certificates from a single location. This ensures that tax is not charged on exempt purchases and allows businesses to respond efficiently to audits.

For this project, my task was to evaluate CertManager’s Customers Page and Exemptions Page to see how it could be improved to serve businesses better.

Challenge

How might we design the Customers and Exemptions Page to better fit customer needs?
We need to solve the following issues:
1. The data on the Customers and Exemptions page take a long time to load
2. Unsure if the information displayed is relevant for user tasks

Project Roadmap

1. Background Info

Competitive Analysis

To understand what the tax exemption space looked like, I conducted competitive analysis on competitors such as Avalara, ExempTax, and Vertex who also had tax exemption products.

2. Gathering Data

Stakeholder Interviews

I conducted interviews with the Manage Services Team to understand how they use CertManager and which columns were most important for their daily tasks.

Surveys on Pendo

Besides interviews, I also set up a pendo survey to gather feedback from customers on what they thought the most important columns were on the Customers/Exemptions page.

Heat map Analytics

In addition to interviews and surveys, I utilized heat map analytics to triangulate qual and quant data to see what information customers most commonly viewed on the Customers and Exemptions page.

Talk to devs

After talking to devs, I learned that 4 separate data joins were conducted in the backend to pull all the data that was needed to display in the Exemptions view.

3. Present Designs

Design Solutions

Consolidating all the information I’ve gathered, I came to the conclusion that 8 columns were needed the most and 3 could be hidden until user expanded search (which wasn’t that common).

Impact

Reflections

1. Being Crafty with B2B Research
The products/projects that I worked on at Sovos were B2B, so I had to be creative with finding ways to gather insight and user feedback. Interviewing customers/end users was not an option due to product nature and time constraints, thus, finding internal users was the way to go.

I discovered that when interviewing internal users (such as the service team and QA team) they are often at a standstill when asked what could be improved about the product. This makes sense as these users use the interface regularly and are experts no matter how complex the system is. I realized that it was most helpful to start with asking how they use the product and ask them to demonstrate how they usually complete certain tasks on the interface to look for pain points.

2. Domain knowledge and getting context
One of the things that I learned during my time at Sovos was picking up on domain knowledge quickly and getting enough context for my projects. I realized that I don’t need to know everything about a product to get started, but rather, I need to figure out what is within the scope of my project and ask questions to get enough context to unblock myself.
