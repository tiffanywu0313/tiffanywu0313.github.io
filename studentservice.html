Responsibilities: I was part of a team of 4 student researchers, one lab coordinator, and one full time ux designer. Everyone participated throughout the research process from June to July 2019. Some of my responsibilities included planning out the research methodology, conducting usability studies, affinity diagram analysis, presenting findings and report writing.



Background

UBC UX Lab is a hands-on program under UBC Student Communication Services where students learn about user experience and conduct experiments to improve the services that UBC provides its students.

I was part of the summer 2019 cohort and I worked on two projects during my time as a UX student researcher, including conducting usability studies on the Transfer credit search tool and the “What can I do with my Science Degree” webpage.

I will be focusing on the “What can I do with my Science Degree” webpage usability study below. You can read more about the Transfer credit search tool project here.

The What can I do with my Science Degree” webpage was launched on May 22, 2019 by UBC Student Communication Services as a resource for students. Our goal was to understand how students are navigating the tool and to find if there are pain points.



Research Question

How are science students navigating the What can I do with my Science Degree” webpage?

This was what the web page looked like as of June 2019. (Note the Keep Exploring section is still an unsorted list of specializations in Science).



Methodology

To evaluate this webpage, we ran usability tests with UBC science students in different year levels that consisted of a survey, a perception test, and a follow up interview.

Usability study flow:

1. Pre-screen Survey: this allowed us to understand the background of each user, such as what year level they are in, their future career prospect, and what they expect on the resource page etc.

2. Perception Test: students were allowed to freely browse the web page for 3-5 min with the talk-aloud method. This allowed us to find the pain points on the web page and take notice of certain functions that were not as intuitive.

3. Follow-up Questions: this allowed us to further question the user on their reaction to the web page and understand why they may have experienced certain issues.

We also used Crazy egg, an analytics software to monitor the activity on the webpage from may to june so we could triangulate the data and find trends in user behavior.



Data Analysis

We conducted an affinity diagram analysis to find pain points and themes on the data collected from 12 undergraduate science students.

Reflection

What I learned

1. Triangulating data is important for finding trends and patterns
Evaluating a solution with different methods helps us determine user behavior by cross-examining. For example, in this research we collected qualitative data from user interviews and quantitative data from web analytics that helped us validate that students are very interested in getting work, research, or volunteer experience.

2. Prioritize feedback
User feedback is good, but don’t depend entirely on them. Users will always have more feature ideas than implementale, or want features that are contradictory. However, it is important for the designer to consider the goal of the platform and prioritize certain features over others.

Limitations and Future Direction

One major limitation we had in this study was that the sample size was quite small compared to the real population of UBC science students. However, according to an article written by Jakob Nielsen from the Nielsen Norman group, running many small tests with five users may be more efficient and potentially more effective than running elaborate usability tests with numerous users.

Another limitation was that the participants recruited for each year level were not representative of the UBC student population. Since the tests were conducted on campus, we were not able to recruit incoming or prospective students. Future usability testing on the page with these groups of students could be beneficial in understanding how these users navigate the page. Further research can also be conducted on the specialization pages with students from those specific specializations.
