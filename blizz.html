Blizz

Blizz is a responsive web experience designed for a plant-based food subscription service for busy modern day people who don’t have time to cook and eat healthy.



Product: Mobile app design

Team: Tiffany Wu, Wesley Yuen, Megha Singhania, Jiasheng Ni

Role: UX Research, UI Design, Report Writing

Tools: Figma

Timeline: January - March 2020

Problem

In this era of information explosion, despite the plethora of travel guides such as blogs and travel websites, it is hard for travelers to find walking routes where they can get a sense of the city while accounting for their interests.

With this in mind, we as a team of 4 students wanted to create a system that allowed users to explore the city at their own pace without having to read through tons of travel blogs and articles as travellers have limited time to spend in a city and don’t have extra time to spend searching.



General Process

1. Empathize: User interviews/surveys, seeking to understand
2. Define: Pain points, prototyping personas, role objectives, challenges
3. Ideate: Share/diverge/converge ideas, determine MVP
4. Prototype: Mockups, simple storyboards, failing fast and iterating quickly
5. Test: Understand problems, what works, what doesn't work, and iterate



Field Study (Empathize + Define)

To learn more about the problem space we conducted a field study to understand how people make walking exploration decisions. During the study, we would first ask participants questions regarding their usual walking exploration in the city, then, in the form of a contextual inquiry, we would ask the participants to decide on a place to go on the spot. We would observe the participant’s behavior and ask questions on the way to their destination.

From our field study, we learned that participants heavily relied on Google Maps and photos to help them come to a decision. In addition, participants often change their minds amidst their exploration and like to have options available to them when they change their minds. We also noticed that they way people new to the city (which we call travellers) and locals have different priorities in walking exploration. Thus, we decided our target users would be travellers and created several personas.



Low-Fidelity Paper Prototype (Ideate + Prototype)

With these takeaways in mind, we designed a rough card deck view prototype that emphasized these traits.

However, after cognitive walkthroughs with several different users, we noticed that the card interface confuses people in terms of navigation.

My team and I wondered if the card deck navigation was too novel and unfamiliar to users and whether a normal list view design would perform better in terms of explorability. Thus, we refined our card deck design and added signifiers that would hopefully make the navigation easier for users; and created a list view prototype so we could compare the two prototypes.



Experimenting with Medium-Fidelity Prototype (Test)

We conducted an experiment to see if one prototype better supports explorability than the other. After performing an ANOVA analysis on the data we collected we were unable to conclude which layout type better supports explorability, however, we were able to identify key features that supported explorability in each of the prototypes. The overview screen in the List view helps users get a sense of what to expect; In the card view, participants enjoyed the larger images and less crowded texts. For future iterations, we can potentially combine the two layouts and let users choose dynamically between the List and a Card view.



Reflection

One of the most challenging parts of this project was during the field study where we observed participants planning and going on a walk. It was hard to know which questions to ask to better understand how users conduct the task even with our pre-prepared list of questions.

It was also challenging to decide on success metrics during the evaluation process to define which prototype performed better. This really came down to what we wanted to prioritize in the user experience of our product, we eventually decided on explorability. This reminded me to not judge an interface design before I understand what problem it is trying to solve first.

The choice of Figma as a prototyping tool may not have been the best. Though Figma offers an online collaborative environment, the limitations in the swiping interaction made testing the prototype challenging. If I were to do this project the second time I would choose to use a different prototyping tool for the card deck prototype. As well, if we had more time and weren’t in the COVID pandemic, we would definitely have tested our prototype on more participants and standardized our experiment process as we had some sessions online and some in person.
